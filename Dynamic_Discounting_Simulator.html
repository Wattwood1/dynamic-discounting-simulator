3 · Scenario Analysis

Scenario A — Supplier Liquidity Stress

Tier-3 (SME) suppliers face a cash crunch and raise their minimum acceptable discount rate by 2–5 percentage points. The buyer earns more per dollar deployed as distressed suppliers compete harder for early payment.

Scenario B — Rising Rate Environment

A central-bank tightening cycle makes bank credit 50% more expensive across the board (approximately +300 bp from a low base). All suppliers raise their minimum rates proportionally, lifting the buyer's annualised yield on the programme.

Scenario C — Capital Sensitivity

Sweeping the capital pool from $1M to $25M reveals a yield cliff: below ~$8M the buyer is capital-constrained and yield is flat; above ~$15M the high-rate SME book is exhausted and yield compresses as cheaper Tier-1 invoices must fill the gap.

5 · Key Insights

  • Tier-3 suppliers are the yield engine. SMEs accept the highest discount rates but have the smallest invoices. The LP funds them first until capital is exhausted, then fills remaining capacity with Tier-2 and finally Tier-1 invoices.
  • Liquidity stress is good for buyers. When SME suppliers need cash urgently, they accept higher discount rates, increasing the buyer's yield with no additional capital commitment.
  • Rising rates lift all tiers. A uniform rate increase (simulating a tightening cycle) improves the buyer's return proportionally across the whole portfolio.
  • Capital sensitivity shows a natural ceiling. Once the buyer's pool exceeds the total SME book, additional capital earns diminishing returns — it must fund lower-rate Tier-1 invoices.
  • The combined stress scenario is the most lucrative for the buyer — higher rates and more urgent suppliers simultaneously maximise discount earned.

6 · Rolling Weekly Auction Model

What changes from the static model

The static LP clears the market once. Real platforms like C2FO run continuous or weekly auctions where:

  • Invoices arrive stochastically — each supplier independently submits a new invoice each week with probability 35%.
  • Funded invoices mature — when an invoice's payment term expires, the buyer is repaid in full (principal + discount), recycling capital back into the pool.
  • Capital compounds — reinvested repayments let the programme grow beyond the initial $10M over the simulation horizon.

We run 26 weeks (≈ one half-year). Each week the LP clears on the live invoice book.

Key dynamics

  • Capital recycling accelerates returns. As short-tenor Tier-3 invoices mature quickly (2–4 weeks), their principal flows back and funds the next cohort — the programme compounds without adding external capital.
  • Steady state reached in ~8 weeks. Active invoice count stabilises as arrivals balance maturities, giving the buyer a predictable monthly income stream.
  • Liquidity stress lifts cumulative discount over baseline, as higher SME rates persist across all 26 auctions.
  • Rising rates compress invoice tenure. Higher cost of waiting means more suppliers accept very short tenors, accelerating capital recycling and increasing weekly throughput.
  • Combined Stress maximises total return — both stress mechanisms active simultaneously produce the highest 26-week discount earned.

7 · Model Limitations & Extensions

LimitationExtension
Partial invoice payments unrealistic for some platforms Add minimum-ticket constraint; binary allocation per supplier (MIP)
Rolling model ignores default risk Add credit-loss term; tier-dependent default probability
Deterministic arrival rate Monte Carlo over arrival probability and invoice size
Single buyer, no competition Model platform marketplace with multiple competing buyers
Static rate preferences Adaptive suppliers who lower rates as liquidity improves
Weekly granularity Daily clearing loop; intra-week capital deployment

Real-World Connections

C2FO — continuous rate auction; our weekly LP approximates each clearing cycle.
